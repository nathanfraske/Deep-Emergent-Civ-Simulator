## Grounding: what the two integrators do, read at source

On main are two integrators feeding the same conservation operator and fan:

- `runout.rs`, the surface-contact law. Its own header states the discipline this arc extends: it is NOT a registry of per-process kernels, it is ONE fixed law that sums the floor force primitives over a parcel's specific-energy budget, and the named surface processes (granular runout, creep, a ballistic-energy throw treated as a surface march) EMERGE as regimes of that one law across the parameter space. The parcel marches cell to cell ALONG the ground, paying `de = g*(drop - mu*cell)` each step, resting when the budget cannot cover the next step. The angle of repose falls out as the break-even `drop = mu*cell`. This already unifies the surface regimes; it is the model the unification follows.
- `ballistic.rs`, the airborne law (arc #177). A closed-form projectile: range `R = v^2*sin(2a)/g`, the parabola `z(x) = x*tan(a) - g*x^2/(2*v^2*cos^2(a))` intersected with the terrain along the azimuth, so the parcel passes OVER intervening relief and lands where the arc first meets the ground. This is exact where it applies and is the non-surface-contact reach the runout march cannot give.

Both are byte-neutral and unarmed: I verified that `runout`, `ballistic`, `launch_fan`, and `ejecta_fan` have NO caller outside their own modules, and no scenario or `run_world` example touches them (they await A's `TransportKernelId` driver arms). So any refactor of them changes no canonical state by construction; the four `run_world` pins cannot move on a change to unarmed primitives. This is load-bearing for the seam below.

The numerics floor this needs is already present: `Fixed::sqrt` (fixed.rs:212) is an exact 128-bit `isqrt` over the shifted radicand, deterministic and identical on every thread count, and the CORDIC `sin_cos`/`atan` are the deterministic angle primitives. So the unification needs no new transcendental; its numerics content is a stepping scheme, not a new function. That is the PD1 correction to my first instinct, which reached for a fixed-point `sqrt` primitive that turned out to already exist.

## The authored template that remains (the reason for the arc)

Runout unifies the surface regimes and ballistic gives the airborne reach, but the CHOICE between them is still authored. A driver, at the arming step, would name which integrator to call: the surface-runout kernel for a granular slump, the ballistic kernel for an impact ejecta throw. That selection reads a high-level process KIND (is this event a slump or a throw) to pick a code path, which is the template case Principle 8 forbids, the same shape the runout header already rejected one level down. A slow slump and a high-energy throw are not two mechanisms; they are the same parcel of mass under different launch energies, and whether it skims the surface or leaves it should EMERGE from that energy and the terrain, never be selected.

The mechanism that removes the selection is a single sub-cell momentum integrator. A parcel carries a velocity vector (a horizontal component and a vertical component, sub-cell position in `Fixed`) rather than a scalar specific-energy budget plus a heading. It is stepped along its path under the floor forces (gravity always; the open `BodyForce` slot for an alien field; Coulomb friction WHILE in contact). The regime is not chosen; it emerges from one geometric test each step: while the parcel's height is at the terrain it is in contact and friction dissipates, and when its upward velocity carries it above the terrain it is airborne and only the body forces act until it descends to meet the ground again. A low-energy parcel never leaves the surface and reproduces the runout march; a high-energy parcel lifts off, flies the ballistic arc, and lands, all from the one law. The surface-contact and airborne regimes become limits of a single integrator, and the driver names an energy and a direction, never a kernel.

This also retires two angular limits I flagged: the surface fan snaps diagonals to the four axes (the cell-march cannot hold a sub-cell heading), and both fans carry a sub-cell floor artifact at the launch. A sub-cell velocity vector holds its heading in `Fixed` across the whole path, so the fan's angular resolution is the CORDIC's, not the grid's, in the contact regime as well as the airborne one.

## The mechanism-shaping seam (surfaced for the gate's ruling)

The seam is the stepping scheme, and it is a real numerics decision, not a detail. The ballistic integrator today is CLOSED-FORM and therefore exact: the parabola is evaluated, not marched, so no discretization error enters. A unified integrator that must transition between contact and flight mid-path cannot stay closed-form, because the lift-off and touch-down points are state-dependent; it must step. So the unification TRADES the ballistic arc's exactness for the generality of one law with an emergent contact boundary. That trade is the honest cost, and the scheme controls how large the cost is.

The stepping I propose is in SPACE along the path, not in time: the parcel advances a bounded step (a fraction of the cell size, the same discretization class as the runout march's cell step and the ballistic march's step cap), and at each step the contact test decides whether friction applies. Space-stepping keeps the scheme in the same determinism envelope the two integrators already live in (a bounded march, never an unbounded until-rest spin), and it makes the airborne segments reduce to the closed-form parabola in the limit of a fine step, so the exactness lost is bounded and measurable against the closed form. A time-stepped scheme (fixed `dt`, semi-implicit) is the alternative; it is the standard symplectic choice and conserves energy well, but it couples the step to the parcel's speed and is a larger departure from the existing marches. I recommend the space-step, and I surface the choice for the gate: space-march (my recommendation, bounded like the existing integrators, reduces to the closed form) or fixed-`dt` symplectic (standard, energy-stable, a larger change). The gate rules the scheme before I build slice 1.

Whichever scheme, the step size is a numerics-and-performance parameter, not a physical one, so it is reserved with its basis, never authored: the step at which the range error against the closed-form parabola falls below the cell size (or below fixed-point tolerance on a flat arc), a numerics bound traded against the per-parcel march cost. I surface it reserved; I do not set it.

## The process decision (surfaced for the gate's ruling)

Because the surface-contact and airborne regimes become limits of the unified integrator, `runout.rs` and `ballistic.rs` become either thin views over it or dead code. Two ways to land it:

- **Retire.** Fold both into the unified integrator: `runout` and `ballistic` are deleted, and the granular-runout and ballistic-throw regimes are proven as limits of the one law (a low-energy launch reproduces the runout deposit, a high-energy launch reproduces the ballistic landing to within the step bound). This is the cleaner Principle-8 outcome, one law with the regime emergent, and it removes the authored selection entirely. It is byte-neutral because nothing arms the two integrators, so no canonical state moves; the four pins hold.
- **Supersede.** Add the unified integrator alongside the two, leave them in place, and migrate A's driver arms to the unified one when they land. This is safer against an unforeseen caller, but it LEAVES the authored kernel selection in the codebase, which is the template the arc exists to remove, so it defeats the arc's purpose if it becomes permanent.

I recommend RETIRE, on the strength of the verified fact that the two integrators are unarmed (no caller, no scenario), so folding them is a pure-refactor with no state change and no pin risk. Superseding only makes sense if the gate wants the closed-form ballistic arc kept as an exact reference oracle for the unified integrator's tests, which is a good reason to keep `ballistic.rs`'s parabola as a test-only exact form even after retiring it from the mechanism path. I surface both; the gate rules whether to retire the siblings or supersede them, and whether to keep the closed-form parabola as a test oracle.

## Emergence, determinism, and admit-the-alien

The regime emerges from geometry (height versus terrain) and the parcel's own velocity, not from a named kind, so no closed enum of regimes is authored. The forces are the floor primitives runout already sums: gravity, the open `BodyForce` slot (an alien parcel driven by a mana-field or redox-potential gradient is a data row, not a rewrite), and Coulomb friction in contact. The parcel keys off its own launch energy, mass, and material friction, so a silicate ejecta clast, an ice grain, and an alien-substance parcel are the same integrator under different data. Determinism holds by the same construction as the two integrators: fixed-point state throughout, a total order on any neighbour or tie decision, a bounded march with a step cap, and energy strictly falling around any contact loop (friction paid every contact step), so the path terminates and is a pure function of the inputs, worker-invariant. The unification is armed by no scenario, so it is byte-neutral against the four `run_world` pins.

## Honest limits

The heightfield is `z = 0` (A's substrate's stated limit), so the integrator is a vertical-plane projection over a 2.5-D surface, not a true 3-D trajectory; that boundary is the substrate's, not this arc's, and the unification does not lift it, it unifies the two laws that live above it. Air drag on the airborne segments is deferred to the floor `drag_force` through the same body-force slot, a refinement (a vacuum or thin-atmosphere world needs none). And the exactness trade is real and named: the closed-form ballistic parabola is exact where it applies, and the unified integrator is general but step-bounded, so the arc buys emergence of the contact boundary at the cost of a bounded, measured discretization error against the closed form. Slice 1's proof measures that error rather than assuming it small.
